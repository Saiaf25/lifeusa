Why We Use Article Outlines

The outline is not meant to make every article sound the same. It is meant to make sure every article has a clear purpose before writing begins.

It protects the reader

The reader should quickly understand what question the article answers, what action they can take, and why LifeUSA is a relevant source.

It protects the writer

The writer should not have to guess the keyword, the audience, the angle, the links, or the limits of what can be claimed.

It protects LifeUSA

The outline catches sensitive claims early, especially around children, medical care, crisis facts, sponsorship, zakat, sadaqah, and program eligibility.

It protects the cluster

Each article should have a job. Some pages answer broad questions. Others answer smaller supporting questions. Together, they help readers and search engines understand LifeUSA's work.

Choose the Format That Fits the Topic

Search research helps us understand what people want, but it should not flatten LifeUSA's voice. A list-style article is useful when the reader wants practical options. A story or feature article is useful when the reader needs to feel the human reality behind a program. Both can belong in the same content cluster.

How We Get From Topic To Outline

  1. Start with a real topic. We begin with a LifeUSA program, campaign, existing article, donor question, meeting note, or keyword opportunity.
  2. Check whether the topic deserves a page. We ask whether people search for it, whether LifeUSA has something real to offer, and whether an existing page already covers it.
  3. Choose the article's job. The page may be a pillar, a supporting article, a story, a feature blog, a campaign update, or an existing article refresh.
  4. Map the reader journey. We write the reader's path before the outline: what they know, what they need next, what might block trust, and what action fits.
  5. Build the section order. Each section should move the reader forward. If two sections do the same job, one should be removed or changed.
  6. Add LifeUSA examples carefully. We connect the topic to real programs, source pages, donation options, reports, or stories without overclaiming.
  7. Separate writing notes from technical notes. Angela should see what helps her write. Technical notes, dead links, and tool details should stay in an appendix or internal note unless they affect the draft.
  8. Review before writing. We check for repeated donation language, unsupported claims, weak transitions, missing links, and sensitive wording.

The Outline Template

Use this structure as a starting point. It can be shortened for simple articles or expanded for major pillar pages.

1. Article Setup

  • Working title: The plain-language article title.
  • Proposed URL: The likely Wix post URL or existing URL to update.
  • Article type: New post, article refresh, pillar, supporting article, story, feature, report-to-blog, or FAQ.
  • Current rank: Whether LifeUSA already ranks for the main query, if data is available.
  • Writer: Who writes the draft.
  • Review owner: Who checks SEO, links, wording, and sensitive claims.

2. Audience And Reader Need

  • Audience: Who is this for?
  • Reader question: What are they trying to understand or do?
  • Reader stage: Learning, comparing, deciding, donating, volunteering, or verifying trust.
  • What would make them leave: Confusing answer, weak proof, repeated donation pitch, vague claim, or missing next step.

3. Keywords And Search Demand

  • Primary keyword: Main search phrase and volume.
  • Secondary keywords: Related phrases and where they fit naturally.
  • Search intent: What the searcher wants from the page.
  • Format signal: What the search results suggest: list, guide, answer, story, definition, or comparison.

4. SERP And Competition

SERP means Search Engine Results Page. In simple terms, it is the Google results page for the main topic or keyword we are studying.

This section explains what Google is already rewarding for the main topic. It is not there to copy competitors. It is there to understand the reader expectation before we choose the article shape.

  • Top competing URLs: The main pages currently ranking.
  • Search result features: Featured snippet, People Also Ask, AI Overview, sitelinks, images, videos, or other visible features.
  • Paid ads: Whether ads appear at the top or bottom of results.
  • Content pattern: Whether ranking pages are lists, guides, stories, definitions, comparisons, charity pages, or forums.
  • Notes about the results page: What the writer should learn from the results.

5. Format Decision

Explain why this article should be written in its chosen shape. This is where we decide whether the article should be a practical list, a careful explanation, a feature story, a historical case study, or an update to an existing article.

Example: A broad search like "how to help orphans" usually needs a practical guide. A topic like a field update from Iraq may work better as a story. Both can link into the same orphan support cluster.

6. Reader Profile And Journey

Define the reader before drafting headings. This keeps the article from becoming a loose list of ideas.

  • Who they are: Donor, supporter, volunteer, partner, researcher, or community member.
  • What they already believe: Their starting point before the article.
  • What they need next: The answer, proof, or action they came for.
  • What could block trust: Missing proof, vague claims, hard sell, or unclear program detail.

Write the reader journey in one line before drafting headings.

I want to help -> what are my options? -> which option is trustworthy? -> what can LifeUSA do? -> what should I do next?

7. Intro Guidance

The intro should answer the reader's real concern quickly. Avoid a generic opening. Explain what the article will help them decide, then move into the first useful section.

  • Hook: The reader's actual problem or question.
  • Promise: What the article will clarify.
  • Trust signal: Why LifeUSA can speak to the topic.
  • Fast start: Do not bury the answer under a long introduction.

8. Section Outline

For each H2 or major section, include:

  • Heading: Clear enough to understand without body text.
  • Why this section is here: The job of the section.
  • Reader stage: What the reader needs at this point.
  • Transition logic: Why this section follows the previous one.
  • Keyword ownership: Which keyword belongs here, if any.
  • What to write: Practical direction for the writer.
  • What to avoid: Repetition, unsupported claims, sensitive wording, or wrong links.
  • LifeUSA connection: Program, story, campaign, or source page.
  • Internal links: Suggested target and anchor text.

9. Internal Links And Next Step

  • Primary internal link: The most important related LifeUSA page.
  • Supporting links: Related articles, program pages, campaign pages, or donation pages.
  • CTA: The action that fits the article. Not every article needs the same donation push.

10. Metadata, FAQ, Images, And Schema

  • Meta title: Clear, search-friendly title.
  • Meta description: One sentence that tells the reader what the article answers.
  • FAQs: Real reader questions, especially where People Also Ask or donor concerns show demand.
  • Image notes: Caption, alt text, or photo direction when the article uses reports, field photos, galleries, or campaign images.
  • Schema notes: FAQ, Article, or other structured data recommendations where useful.

11. Information Gain

This is where we explain what LifeUSA can add that competing pages do not already give the reader.

  • Unique LifeUSA proof: Program examples, countries, campaign history, field updates, reports, or donation pathways.
  • Clearer explanation: A simpler answer to a question competitors make confusing.
  • Better reader path: A more useful order, checklist, comparison, or decision guide.
  • Original angle: The LifeUSA-specific point of view, not just a rewritten search result.

12. Internal Appendix

Keep this separate from the main writer instructions unless it affects what Angela should write.

  • Source notes: Files, URLs, emails, meeting notes, or research used.
  • URL checks: Live links, redirects, 404s, noindex pages, and pages to avoid.
  • Tool notes: Keyword source, Ahrefs checks, Google Search Console, or crawl evidence.
  • Open questions: Claims that need LifeUSA approval before publishing.

Review Checklist Before Sending To Draft

  • Does the article have a clear job in the cluster?
  • Does the format fit the topic, not just the keyword?
  • Does the first section answer the reader quickly?
  • Does the search results section explain the format decision?
  • Does the article add information gain beyond competing pages?
  • Does each section move the reader forward?
  • Are LifeUSA examples specific and sourced?
  • Are any child, medical, crisis, sponsorship, zakat, or sadaqah claims safe to publish?
  • Are internal links live and relevant?
  • Is the writer free to use voice, story, and creativity within the article's purpose?
Plain rule: The outline should guide the writing, not handcuff it. If the best article is a story, make it a story. If the best article is a list, make it a list. The reason for choosing the format should always be clear.
